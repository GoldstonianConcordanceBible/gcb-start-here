Many people treat “the gospel” like it starts in the New Testament.

But the gospel is not a sudden announcement with no backstory.

It’s the **completion of a covenant storyline** that starts earlier than most people realize.

## The gospel is covenant fulfillment
The Bible is not mainly a self-help guide.

It is the record of:
- a promise made
- a people formed
- a failure revealed
- a rescue prepared
- a Messiah provided
- a Kingdom announced
- a new creation guaranteed

The Gospels are not disconnected biographies.

They are the moment the storyline becomes a person.

## Why the beginning matters
When you disconnect the gospel from covenant, you reduce it.

You turn it into:
- “God forgives you” (true, but incomplete)
- “You can go to heaven” (hopeful, but partial)
- “Jesus helps your life” (often shallow)

Covenant restores the depth:

The gospel is the announcement that **God has kept His promise**—and the Kingdom is arriving in the Messiah.

## What “good news” actually means
Good news is news that a King has acted.

That means the gospel includes:
- forgiveness
- reconciliation
- deliverance
- lordship
- a new community
- a promised future

If Jesus is only Savior, people try to use Him.

If Jesus is Savior and Lord, people learn to follow Him.

## A practical way to read the Gospels
When reading Matthew, Mark, Luke, or John, ask:

- What promises are being fulfilled here?
- What covenant patterns are being repeated here?
- What is Jesus revealing about God’s Kingdom?

That shifts you from “story mode” to “fulfillment mode.”
